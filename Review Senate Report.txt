## Analysis of Senate Veterans' Affairs Committee Report on Khamisiyah Chemical Exposure

The document is a section from the U.S. Senate Committee on Veterans' Affairs report examining the Khamisiyah incident during the 1991 Gulf War, with particular focus on how the Department of Defense (DoD) and CIA modeled and assessed potential chemical weapons exposure to American troops.

### Key Findings About Intelligence Failures

The report identifies significant intelligence failures preceding the Khamisiyah incident. In May 1986, intelligence sources indicated chemical weapons were moved to Khamisiyah during the Iran-Iraq war. A November 1986 CIA assessment concluded that chemical weapons were stored "at the southern forward ammunition depot located at Tall al Lahm." However, analysts kept Khamisiyah off chemical weapons facility lists because it lacked "S-shaped bunkers" which were considered characteristic of chemical weapons storage. This intelligence bias toward looking only for S-shaped bunkers led to critical errors in pre-war assessment.

### Analysis of DoD/CIA Modeling Efforts

The report criticizes the DoD and CIA's joint efforts to model chemical agent release from the March 10, 1991 pit demolition at Khamisiyah. It notes that what the agencies termed a "plume" was actually "a mathematically derived computer model used to produce a theory" rather than an observed phenomenon. The Senate committee explains that without on-site measurements of actual chemical releases, the models relied on reconstructed weather data and assumptions about dispersion patterns.

The committee identified "numerous flaws in the methodology" used in the DoD/CIA plume analysis that "make it questionable to rely on" for determining exposure levels. Their review concluded that the modeling "likely overestimated by a considerable degree the probable area in which sarin and cyclosarin" could have been dispersed. Despite these limitations, the results were published in July 1997 and used to notify approximately 100,000 Gulf War veterans of potential exposure.

### Methodological Concerns With Testing

The committee found significant problems with the Dugway Proving Ground tests conducted to simulate the Khamisiyah demolition:

1. The test failed to accurately recreate the pit's configuration, omitting a key wall ("berm") that would have absorbed some of the explosive force and chemical agent
2. Testing occurred on a flat test range that didn't replicate the pit's micro-atmospheric conditions
3. Equipment was positioned too close to the detonation with no attempt to track the plume beyond several hundred yards
4. Dugway personnel used a methodical approach to placing charges rather than replicating the "random, almost haphazard technique" described by EOD personnel at Khamisiyah
5. The test inexplicably included concrete-filled rocket cases with no rationale provided despite committee inquiries

### Alternative Modeling Results

The committee discovered that the Air Force Technical Assistance Center (AFTAC) had produced an alternative plume model that differed significantly from the DoD/CIA version. At the committee's request, AFTAC analyzed the same Khamisiyah incident and produced results showing "a plume track that moved south and then east over portions of northern Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, and the Persian Gulf" - covering an area that included Navy personnel on ships and Kuwaiti civilians, but was not reflected in the DoD/CIA model.

The committee noted that AFTAC "is well regarded among modeling agencies employed by DOD as a source of sound atmospheric modeling techniques" and their peer-reviewed analysis had "substantial merit," deepening concerns about the reliability of the official DoD/CIA model.

### Evolving Exposure Estimates

The report documents how Pentagon estimates of troops potentially exposed to Khamisiyah chemical releases changed dramatically over time:
- In 1996: Officials claimed no U.S. troops were exposed
- By early 1997: Revised to state 20,000 personnel possibly exposed
- July 1997: Computer modeling increased estimate to approximately 100,000 troops
- Early 1998: Suggestion numbers could exceed 110,000

### Conclusions and Criticisms

The committee concluded that "the theoretical model of the Khamisiyah demolition presented in July 1997 by DOD and the CIA was fundamentally flawed." It lacked adequate peer review, with the only review occurring "well after the report had been publicly released" and consisting of "a single session lasting less than two days."

While the committee found "from available evidence there does not appear to have been a concerted attempt by the government to suppress the facts surrounding the Khamisiyah destruction," it determined that "the failure for years to investigate fully once the allegations of presence of chemical weapons agent in proximity to U.S. troops were made was at least negligent and should not happen again."

The report is particularly critical of the DoD's decision to notify veterans based on flawed modeling, noting that when questioned about rushing the release, DoD officials stated "we had an obligation to make our findings public... to reduce the uncertainty many veterans were feeling." The committee suggests this approach may have "ended up doing more to confuse and alarm Gulf War veterans, both those healthy and ill, than to help them."

This portion of the Senate report demonstrates significant concerns about the scientific validity of the chemical exposure assessments that formed the basis for veteran notifications and subsequent healthcare decisions. The committee's investigation suggests methodological flaws, premature release of unvalidated models, and a failure to incorporate alternative analyses that showed different exposure patterns.
